13:T692,<p>I was gifted a Raspberry Pi 5 not too long ago, and it sat idle for a bit, I finally decided to put it to use.</p>
<p>One of the coolest things about the Pi 5 is its support for <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raspberry_Pi#Add-on_boards_(HATs)">HATs</a> (Hardware Attached on Top). There are tons to choose from, but one caught my eye: a HAT that combines <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_over_Ethernet">PoE</a> and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NVM_Express">NVMe SSD</a> storage. Since I've had microSD cards fail on me in the past, upgrading to NVMe feels like a solid move for reliability and speed. And with PoE, I can power the Pi and provide internet through a single Ethernet cable - keeping the setup clean and simple.</p>
<p><img src="/images/raspberrypi5.jpg" alt=""></p>
<p><em>A <a href="https://pipci.jeffgeerling.com/hats/geeekpi-p33-m2-nvme-poe-hat.html">GeeekPi P33 HAT</a> on my Raspberry Pi 5, powered by an Ethernet cable.</em></p>
<p>I recently snagged <a href="https://store.ui.com/us/en/products/us-8-150w">a used 8-port 150w Ubiquiti PoE switch</a>. It's a bit older, but perfect for powering a few of my PoE devices including this.</p>
<p>Next up: I'm planning to connect the Pi to an RTL-SDR antenna to track planes flying overhead using ADS-B signals. Being in Seattle, I get plenty of SeaTac airport traffic right above me, so there's always something interesting to monitor. I'll also run a few lightweight Docker containers alongside for experimenting.</p>
<p>Eventually, as I continue my Kubernetes journey - this Pi will eventually join the cluster as a node. I'm excited for all tinkering to come with this device!</p>
